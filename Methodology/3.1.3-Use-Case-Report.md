# 3.1.3 Use Case Report

This section presents the use case reports for the main LYDO Connect functionalities shown in the updated general use case diagram. The report follows the reference format by identifying the use case ID, use case name, scenario, triggering event, brief description, actors, included use cases, extended use cases, pre-conditions, post-conditions, flow of events, alternative flow, and exception conditions.

The updated use case reports are aligned with the current general diagram scope: account access, public portal viewing, youth participation services, organization information posting, citizen desk services, admin dashboard access, administrative record management, transparency and financial management, user and role administration, and audit log review. Supporting behaviors such as validation, active session checking, searching, filtering, exporting, and status updating are treated as included or extended use cases rather than separate main use case reports.

## Thematic Analysis of Foreign Literature

### Theme 1: Digital Platforms and Youth Engagement in Local Governance Contexts

Digital platforms are increasingly used as participation environments where young people engage in civic expression, consultation, and issue-based public interaction. Ramos et al. (2025), Weiss (2020), and Neag et al. (2024) collectively describe youth engagement as multi-form and context-dependent, where formal governance participation, informal digital expression, and platform-mediated activism coexist. Across these works, the core framing is that youth engagement cannot be treated as a single behavior; it involves different participant groups, varying levels of institutional connection, and distinct forms of online and offline action. This is directly relevant to youth-governance systems because platform design must reflect who participates, how they participate, and what institutional pathways convert engagement into governance-facing input.

Ramos et al. (2025) synthesize evidence showing a recurring gap between participation opportunities and democratic outcomes, especially when participation processes are not linked to accountable institutional action. Weiss (2020) explains that youth political participation repertoires are diversified, meaning young people may engage through nontraditional, issue-driven, or digitally mediated channels rather than only through formal institutional formats. Neag et al. (2024) further shows that children and youth digital activism is shaped by inclusion conditions, platform affordances, and context-specific barriers, reinforcing that engagement quality depends on how participation spaces are structured and recognized. For the present study, these findings justify a system design that records not only participation events but also participation pathways, user roles, and follow-through indicators so that engagement data can be interpreted in governance terms instead of being reduced to activity counts.

A key limitation across these literature sources is that they provide strong conceptual and synthesis-level understanding of youth engagement patterns but limited operational guidance on how local youth offices should implement integrated participation workflows in one digital platform. The studies explain participation diversity and participation-outcome disconnection, but they do not define concrete data structures, tracking logic, or institutional process integration models for office-level implementation. This creates a direct gap for LYDO-oriented systems, where participation records must be consistently encoded, linked to administrative actions, and monitored as part of local governance routines. The proposed system addresses this gap by organizing youth-engagement processes in a centralized structure designed for participation visibility, process continuity, and measurable governance relevance.

### Theme 2: E-Governance, Transparency, and Public-Value Conditions

E-governance transparency is effective only when institutions move beyond isolated publication practices and adopt coordinated digital governance systems. Twizeyimana and Andersson (2019), Matheus and Janssen (2019/2020), and Lourenço (2023) frame transparency as an institutional and service-quality condition, not merely a data-release task. Their shared emphasis is that public value emerges when records, services, and governance information are organized through reliable digital arrangements that support usability, trust, and accountability. In local governance environments, this is critical because transparency obligations often involve recurring updates, cross-functional records, and citizen-facing information access requirements that require process coherence.

Twizeyimana and Andersson (2019) identify public-value dimensions of e-government that include service improvement, administrative efficiency, and open-government capability, indicating that value depends on coordinated system design rather than disconnected tools. Matheus and Janssen (2019/2020) show that transparency through open government data is limited when publication is not accompanied by accessibility, interpretability, and practical reuse conditions. Lourenço (2023) reinforces this through a transparency framework perspective that aligns public e-service transparency with user-oriented information requirements and governance clarity. For the present study, these findings support a platform model that integrates participation records, transparency documents, and governance-relevant service information in one environment so that local disclosure is understandable, accessible, and operationally linked to service processes.

The main gap across these literature sources is that they establish strong governance-level principles for transparency and public value but provide limited office-level implementation detail for specialized youth-governance operations. They do not fully specify how a local youth office should structurally integrate participation data, service records, and transparency outputs in a single workflow architecture with routine maintenance and traceability controls. This limitation is significant in LYDO settings, where transparency and youth-service functions are interdependent in practice but often fragmented across separate channels. ### Theme 3: PWA-Based Accessibility and Technology Implementation for Local Public Services

Progressive Web Applications are positioned in the literature as a practical technology path for delivering cross-device public services with lower deployment friction and broader reach. Fauzan et al. (2025), Roumeliotis and Tselikas (2022), and Ara et al. (2023/2024) collectively indicate that technical adoption decisions should be evaluated against usability, accessibility, and quality-assurance requirements, especially for public-facing systems. Their combined framing is that platform technology is not only an engineering choice but also an access and service-equity decision, because technical architecture influences whether users with varied devices and capability conditions can consistently access and complete service tasks.

Fauzan et al. (2025) synthesize PWA practices and challenges, highlighting both implementation benefits and recurrent constraints that affect reliability and maintainability in real deployments. Roumeliotis and Tselikas (2022) evaluate accessibility implications in PWA contexts, emphasizing that inclusive support for persons with disabilities must be designed and tested rather than assumed from technology choice alone. Ara et al. (2023/2024) strengthens this through accessibility-engineering review insights that stress systematic evaluation criteria, conformance-oriented checks, and usability-quality verification in web systems. For the present study, these findings support adopting a PWA architecture for LYDO workflows while embedding accessibility and quality controls as integral design requirements, especially for users with varying digital skills, device conditions, and service-access constraints.

The shared limitation across these literature sources is that they provide broad technology and accessibility guidance but do not deliver a complete implementation blueprint for youth-governance service integration in local office settings. They do not fully define how PWA architecture should be operationally combined with participation tracking, transparency modules, and service-request workflows in one governance-oriented application structure. This leaves a practical design gap for local youth-governance systems that must balance technical feasibility, accessibility compliance, and administrative process integration. The proposed system addresses this gap by applying PWA-based delivery in a unified LYDO platform with role-aware workflows, structured records, and accessibility-oriented interface behavior to support consistent and inclusive local digital service execution.
