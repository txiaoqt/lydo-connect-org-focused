## Theme 1: Digital Platforms and Youth Engagement
### Existing Manuscript Text (Unchanged)
Digital Platforms and Youth Engagement
Digital platforms have become important tools in strengthening youth civic engagement and participation in governance activities. Boulianne (2019) found that digital media positively influences civic and political participation among young people by making engagement more accessible and interactive. Similarly, Theocharis and van Deth (2018) explained that online participation expands traditional civic engagement through digital consultations and e-participation systems, allowing youth to participate beyond conventional face-to-face activities. Kahne and Bowyer (2018) further emphasized that digital environments help develop participatory skills and political efficacy among youth, strengthening their involvement in democratic processes. These studies are relevant to the proposed study because they support the idea that a centralized digital platform can encourage greater youth participation within Local Youth Development Offices.
Studies also highlight that accessibility and usability significantly affect the success of digital engagement platforms. Hargittai et al. (2018) noted that disparities in internet access and digital literacy continue to limit participation despite the availability of digital systems. Mesa (2023) similarly found that differences in digital skills, education, and access conditions influence how individuals engage with e-government platforms. These findings are important to the proposed study because they emphasize the need for a user-friendly and accessible system that accommodates users with varying levels of digital capability.
Research on e-government systems further demonstrates the importance of digital platforms in improving transparency and public participation. Valle et al. (2024) found that digital government platforms improve citizen engagement and service delivery in local government units, although issues related to security and trust remain concerns among users. Likewise, Matias and Vigonte (2025) explained that fragmented government systems reduce the effectiveness of digital governance initiatives and create barriers to accessibility and transparency. These studies suggest that centralized and integrated digital platforms are necessary to improve communication, information access, and participation in local governance settings such as LYDOs.
The role of structured participation systems in youth governance was also emphasized in several studies. Lastimado et al. (2025) found that youth participation activities in local governance often rely on manual attendance systems and informal communication methods, making participation difficult to monitor and sustain. Similarly, Bulusan (2025) identified weak engagement structures and the absence of organized digital systems as major barriers to youth participation in barangay-level governance. These findings are directly related to the proposed system because they reveal the need for a centralized platform capable of organizing participation records, public content, and communication processes.
Studies on Progressive Web Applications (PWAs) also support the technical direction of the proposed study. Fauzan et al. (2022) explained that PWAs improve accessibility through faster loading speeds, offline functionality, and cross-platform compatibility. In addition, Biørn-Hansen et al. (2019) found that PWAs combine the accessibility of web systems with the functionality of mobile applications, making them suitable for public-sector digital platforms. These studies are relevant because the proposed LYDO system aims to provide accessible digital services across desktops, tablets, and mobile devices without requiring native application installation.
Overall, the reviewed studies demonstrate that digital platforms can significantly improve youth engagement, transparency, participation monitoring, and governance accessibility when systems are designed to be centralized, interactive, and user-centered. However, the literature also reveals recurring gaps such as fragmented participation systems, limited accessibility, weak transparency mechanisms, and the absence of youth-focused integrated governance platforms. These gaps justify the development of the proposed Progressive Web Application for Local Youth Development Offices, which aims to provide a unified platform for information access, participation monitoring, and transparent youth governance.

## Theme 2: E-Governance and Government Transparency
### Existing Manuscript Text (Unchanged)
E-Governance and Government Transparency
The legal and institutional foundations of e-governance in the Philippines establish clear expectations for transparency, digital service delivery, and public accountability at all levels of government. Republic Act No. 12254, or the E-Governance Act, mandates that government agencies maintain formal, secure, accessible, and regularly updated information portals, while Republic Act No. 11032, or the Ease of Doing Business and Efficient Government Service Delivery Act of 2018, demands the streamlining of public services through integrated digital systems (Macabare et al., 2025). Despite these mandates, research consistently reveals that local government units have yet to operationalize these requirements in a meaningful way. Del Rosario et al. (2025) identify digital transformation gaps at the local level as a primary obstacle to inclusive governance, noting that many offices remain reliant on static or fragmented digital tools that fall far short of the interactive, functional portals envisioned by law. Matias and Vigonte (2025a, 2025b) similarly found that the journey from governance vision to digital reality is frequently stalled by a lack of infrastructure and institutional readiness within local government units, while Capuno and Orbeta (2007) established that government websites often fail as transparency tools due to poor user-centric design and inconsistent content updates. These findings collectively expose a persistent and systemic gap between what Philippine e-governance law requires and what local offices are actually delivering.
This institutional gap is further compounded by failures in data governance and information quality — conditions that directly undermine the transparency objectives that e-governance frameworks are designed to achieve. Twizeyimana and Andersson (2019) argue that e-government produces genuine public value through improved service delivery, administrative efficiency, and transparency only when systems operate in a coordinated and integrated manner, and that fragmented digital infrastructures significantly reduce the effectiveness and credibility of digital governance. Purwanto et al. (2020) reinforce this by demonstrating that citizens' trust in open government data is shaped by data quality and system reliability, and that unreliable or inconsistent information weakens public trust in digital platforms. In Philippine contexts, these problems are observable at the operational level. Valenzuela (2025) notes that manual record-keeping makes data difficult to analyze and limits administrators' capacity to monitor program outcomes, while Mao and Zhu (2025) establish that the use of multiple non-integrated tools results in inconsistent records and data gaps that weaken service delivery and decision-making. These conditions are directly present in Local Youth Development Offices (LYDOs), where participation records, transparency documents, and program-related data remain dispersed across social media platforms, manual files, and disconnected third-party tools — a situation fundamentally incompatible with the coordinated digital governance envisioned under Republic Act No. 12254.
The transparency and accountability deficits identified in the broader e-governance literature are particularly acute in youth governance offices, where the absence of formal digital mechanisms has allowed informal workarounds to substitute for institutionalized accountability practices. Bulusan (2025) found that Sangguniang Kabataan (SK) officials, in the absence of formal transparency channels, relied on social media and informal task delegation — strategies that are neither sustainable nor consistent with open governance standards. OroNa-Cabagay et al. (2025) reinforced the severity of this problem by identifying persistent barriers to Right to Information (RTI) implementation in the Philippines, including bureaucratic complexity, technological limitations, and institutional resistance, all of which prevent citizens from accessing information on government programs. Ong and Gabriel (2018) further established a statistically significant positive relationship between transparency adherence and legislative performance, and explicitly recommended ICT adoption as a means of strengthening monitoring and public responsiveness. Evidence from local e-government deployments confirms that digital platforms can effectively address these gaps when properly implemented. Macabare et al. (2025) found that Quezon City's QC E-Services Portal demonstrates how local-level digitization can improve public access to information consistent with the mandates of Republic Act No. 12254, while Barrios and Moreno (2024) demonstrated that e-services portals in Zamboanga City reduced processing time, improved administrative transparency, and supported more efficient government service management. However, Bokingkito et al. (2025) found that Philippine government websites recorded a 0% compliance rate at the connected services stage, and Valle et al. (2024) noted that fewer than 60% of LGU portal users trusted platform security — underscoring that transparency through digital governance remains an unresolved institutional challenge.
Despite the breadth of literature documenting e-governance gaps and transparency deficits, no existing study proposes or evaluates a digital platform specifically designed to address these needs within youth-focused government offices. Bulusan (2025) calls for digital governance tools for SK officials but stops short of designing one. OroNa-Cabagay et al. (2025) document RTI barriers without offering a platform-level solution applicable to LYDOs. Ong and Gabriel (2018) recommend ICT adoption broadly without addressing the operational mandates of youth-specific offices. Valle et al. (2024) and Barrios and Moreno (2024) evaluate general LGU service portals that do not account for youth-specific functions such as participation monitoring and local youth organization registration under the Youth Organizations' Registration Program (YORP). Twizeyimana and Andersson (2019) and Purwanto et al. (2020) remain largely conceptual without proposing practical systems for integrating participation and transparency data within a single governance platform. This convergence of unaddressed institutional needs directly justifies the development of the proposed system, which consolidates program information, participation records, and accountability reports into a single, publicly accessible platform — translating the policy imperatives and legal mandates documented across these studies into a functional, youth-specific digital solution for Local Youth Development Offices in Metro Manila.

### Transition Add-on (Inserted Missing RRLs)
To extend this transparency theme, service-quality and portal-design literature adds concrete implementation factors. Chan et al. (2020), Alkraiji (2020), and Singh et al. (2022) consistently indicate that usability, information quality, privacy, security, and trust are critical predictors of e-government satisfaction and sustained usage. At the governance layer, OECD (2020), Bertot et al. (2016), Nam (2018), and Meijer et al. (2018) emphasize openness, accountability, and participatory institutional design as core outcomes of digital government, while Linders (2017) cautions that many systems remain one-way communication channels unless interactivity is explicitly designed.
Operational and local evidence further supports these requirements. Ghodousi et al. (2019) demonstrate that structured complaint datasets can improve service evaluation and prioritization. Kiram et al. (2024) underscore that data governance and privacy protections are necessary for trust in digital public services. Gabuya (2025) identifies readiness, infrastructure, and training constraints in legislative digitization, while Espiritu et al. (2023) report similar implementation barriers in LGU digital transformation, including budget, skills, and cybersecurity concerns. Together, these studies strengthen the case for a transparent, integrated, and realistically implementable LYDO governance platform.

## Theme 3: Progressive Web Application and Accessibility
### Existing Manuscript Text (Unchanged)
Progressive Web Application and Accessibility
Progressive Web Applications have become relevant tools in improving accessibility and usability in digital public service platforms. Mole (2020) explained that PWAs provide a cross-platform solution by combining the wide reach of web applications with the functionality of mobile applications. This is important in digital governance because users access public services through different devices, internet conditions, and levels of technical capability. These findings are relevant to the proposed study because the LYDO system aims to provide accessible services without requiring users to install a native mobile application.
Studies also emphasize that accessibility remains a major concern in e-government systems. Mesa (2023) found that digital divide conditions, including internet access, digital skills, usage patterns, and educational background, influence how individuals engage with digital government platforms. Similarly, Bokingkito et al. (2025) found that many Philippine e-government websites still experience accessibility challenges, especially for users with older devices or disabilities. These studies show that the availability of an online system does not automatically ensure meaningful access, making it necessary for digital platforms to be designed with inclusivity and usability in mind.
Research on PWAs further supports their usefulness in addressing these accessibility issues. Fauzan et al. (2022) explained that PWAs improve user access through faster loading speed, offline functionality, and optimized performance. Likewise, Biørn-Hansen et al. (2019) found that PWAs combine the accessibility of web systems with mobile application features, making them suitable for users who rely on smartphones, tablets, or desktop devices. These studies are important to the proposed system because they support the use of PWA technology as a practical approach for making LYDO services available across different devices and network conditions.
User-centered design is also an important factor in the success of PWAs and other digital public systems. Malavolta et al. (2018) highlighted that PWAs can reduce development costs while maintaining cross-platform compatibility, while Al-Debei et al. (2020) emphasized that usability and performance influence user adoption of digital platforms. In addition, studies on user-centered design show that systems designed around actual user needs improve engagement and satisfaction. However, Mergel (2016) noted that many public-sector platforms fail to apply modern design principles, resulting in poor usability and low user engagement.
Overall, the reviewed studies show that Progressive Web Applications can improve accessibility in digital governance by providing cross-platform access, faster performance, offline capabilities, and mobile-friendly functionality. However, the literature also reveals continuing gaps such as digital divide barriers, limited accessibility in Philippine e-government websites, poor usability, and weak user-centered implementation. These gaps support the need for a PWA-based LYDO platform that can reduce technical barriers, improve service reach, and ensure that youth users can access government information and services more consistently across different digital environments.
