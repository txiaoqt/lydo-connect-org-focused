## Claims That Need Rewording If You Want Strictly PH-Based RRL Only

The following parts of the current Statement of the Problem are risky if you want every line to be defensible using only Philippine-based RRL already found locally:

1. `Many LYDOs rely on social media platforms as their primary channel`
   - Why risky: The current local sources support communication issues, but they do not directly establish that many LYDOs primarily rely on social media
   - Safer wording:
   - `Youth communication and information dissemination in local governance are often carried out through social media, manual coordination, and other fragmented channels.`

2. `Social media is not a dependable channel for official government communication`
   - Why risky: This is strongly supported by the foreign study `Villodre (2024)`, but not directly by the Philippine-based sources currently present in the local draft
   - Safer wording:
   - `Reliance on social media and other informal digital channels alone may result in ineffective communication, inconsistent access to information, and weak traceability of official records.`

3. `Most offices lack a centralized platform`
   - Why risky: This may be true, but the current local RRL does not directly measure "most offices"
   - Safer wording:
   - `Some local youth governance settings still lack a centralized digital platform for managing and publishing information.`

## Recommended Revised Statement of the Problem

The version below is written so that its claims are more safely supported by the Philippine-based RRL already present in the paper.

`Local Youth Development Offices (LYDOs) play an important role in implementing youth programs, supporting youth participation, and promoting accountability in local governance. However, existing studies and local governance contexts show several gaps that may affect the effectiveness of these functions. First, youth access to information may become inconsistent when communication and dissemination are handled through fragmented channels such as social media, manual coordination, and disconnected digital tools. This can limit youth awareness of available programs, services, and participation opportunities. Second, the monitoring of youth engagement and program outcomes remains a challenge. Without a more structured mechanism for recording participation and assessing program implementation, LYDOs may experience difficulty in identifying engagement trends, evaluating outcomes, and improving youth initiatives based on evidence. Third, transparency and public access to information may also be weakened when governance records and reports are not organized in a centralized and accessible platform. Since public offices are expected to provide accessible information and uphold accountability, the absence of a structured digital system can reduce public visibility, weaken trust, and limit the overall effectiveness of youth governance services. Taken together, these gaps highlight the need for a centralized web-based platform that can improve information dissemination, support monitoring of youth participation and program outcomes, and strengthen transparency in local youth governance.`
