## Revised Statement of the Problem with RRL Citations

Local Youth Development Offices (LYDOs) play an important role in organizing youth programs, encouraging participation, and supporting accountable youth governance at the local level. However, Philippine and Philippines-focused studies suggest that several gaps continue to affect how local youth governance connects with the youth and with the public. Studies on Sangguniang Kabataan and youth participation show that youth involvement is not always sustained or evenly distributed, and that participation is shaped by institutional credibility, communication, and the actual opportunities available to young people beyond formal youth leadership positions (Bautista, 2020; Alampay & Angeles, 2012; Castillo et al., 2024).

First, youth access to information and participation opportunities may become inconsistent when communication and engagement depend on fragmented channels rather than a structured system. Philippines-focused studies on e-participation and youth governance show that digital interaction alone does not automatically create meaningful access or sustained engagement, especially when communication is weak, informal, or not translated into clear participation mechanisms (Huffman, 2017; Bautista, 2020; Castillo et al., 2024). Studies of digital governance in the Philippines also show that existing government web platforms still suffer from quality, inclusivity, and public engagement gaps, which suggests that access to information remains uneven when digital services are not well designed and centralized (Lagura, 2025).

Second, the monitoring of youth engagement and program outcomes remains a challenge. Research on LYDO operations and youth leadership in the Philippines points to the need for stronger mechanisms for tracking participation, assessing program implementation, and identifying the actual strengths and weaknesses of youth initiatives (Valenzuela, 2025; Reguindin, 2023; Valencia, 2023). Without a more systematic platform for recording participation and organizing program records, local youth offices may struggle to evaluate outcomes, detect engagement patterns, and improve programs based on evidence.

Third, transparency and public access to governance information may also be weakened when records are not managed in a centralized and accessible digital environment. Philippine studies on local governance consistently connect transparency and accountability with public trust and public service delivery (Ong & Gabriel, 2018; Gabriel et al., 2019). At the same time, Philippine studies on FOI and e-participation show that access-to-information systems can still fail when design, responsiveness, and user support are inadequate (Perez, 2020; Tan & Lum, 2025). Studies on local government web systems in the Philippines further show that better-designed web-based platforms can improve transparency, accessibility, and service delivery, which supports the need for a dedicated local youth governance system rather than fragmented records and disconnected channels (Barrios & Moreno, 2024; Albano, 2024; Lagura, 2025).

Taken together, these studies indicate a broader problem: local youth governance processes may remain fragmented across informal communication, disconnected records, and weakly integrated digital practices, making it difficult to connect information dissemination, youth participation, program monitoring, and transparency in one coherent workflow (Huffman, 2017; Valenzuela, 2025; Perez, 2020; Barrios & Moreno, 2024). This highlights the need for a centralized web-based platform that can provide reliable access to youth information, support participation and monitoring, and strengthen transparency and accountability in local youth governance.
