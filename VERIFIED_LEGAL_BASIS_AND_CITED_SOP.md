## Important Distinction

For your paper, laws and executive issuances do not function the same way as Review of Related Literature (RRL).

- `RRL` supports your empirical and conceptual claims using prior studies.
- `Laws and issuances` support your legal mandate, compliance requirements, system boundaries, and policy justification.

So yes, the laws are important, but they should usually be cited as `legal basis`, `policy basis`, `compliance basis`, or `statutory mandate`, not as substitutes for all literature.

## Corrections You Should Make

### 1. RA 10742 is important, but RA 11768 should now be cited with it

`RA 10742` created the Local Youth Development Office, while `RA 11768`, approved on `May 6, 2022`, strengthened the law and amended some provisions. Based on the verified law text:

- `RA 10742` provides for the creation of a Youth Development Office in every province, city, and municipality
- `RA 11768` amended the LYDO provisions and states that a local youth development officer shall be appointed within one year from the effectivity of the amendatory law
- `RA 11768` also states that the LGU may appropriate `not less than one percent (1%)` of its annual budget for the Local Youth Development Office

This makes `RA 11768` highly useful for the claim that youth governance should have a formal and functional local office structure with planning and operational support.

### 2. `YORP` and `LYDP` are better anchored in the IRR, not only in Sections 25 and 26

From the verified `2016 IRR of RA 10742`:

- `LYDP` is defined in the IRR as the provincial, city, or municipal youth development plan drafted by the SK Pederasyon, finalized by the LYDC, and approved by the local sanggunian
- the IRR also states that the `CBYDP` must be anchored on the `PYDP` and other `LYDPs`
- local registration and verification of youth organizations and youth-serving organizations are handled by the `LYDO` or designated LGU personnel
- the IRR also lists LYDO functions that are subject to the revitalized `YORP` guidelines

So if you want to justify:

- youth organization registration
- local youth development planning
- structured youth governance records

then the IRR is a very strong source for those claims.

### 3. `EO No. 2, s. 2016` is useful, but do not overstate its coverage

This is important. `EO No. 2` operationalizes the people’s right to information in the `Executive Branch`. It is useful for:

- your transparency portal concept
- public access to information
- disclosure-oriented design

But for an LGU-based youth system, `EO No. 2` should be treated as a `supportive transparency policy basis`, not the only legal basis for disclosure duties. To strengthen this part of your paper, it is better paired with:

- the `1987 Constitution`, Article III, Section 7 on the right to information
- the `1987 Constitution`, Article II, Section 28 on full public disclosure
- `RA 6713`, especially the duty to make public documents accessible

### 4. `RA 10175` is useful, but mostly as a secondary security law

This law is relevant because it penalizes:

- illegal access
- data interference
- system interference
- computer-related fraud and identity theft

It can support the claim that the system must be designed with safeguards against unlawful access and misuse. However, it is not your best primary basis for system design. For system design and handling of personal data, `RA 10173` and `RA 12254` are more central.

For `cyber-libel`, use this only if your system allows user-generated public content. If your `Citizen Desk` is a controlled, case-based, or private submission channel, then `cyber-libel` is not a central legal anchor for the paper.

### 5. `DICT Memorandum Circular No. 004, s. 2017` is usable for accessibility claims

Using the PDF link you provided, this memorandum circular is now verified as a usable accessibility basis. It is helpful because it prescribes the Philippine Web Accessibility Policy and adopts `WCAG 2.0` as a standard for making web content more accessible to a wider range of persons with disabilities.

This makes it a good legal support for:

- accessible government website design
- inclusivity of digital public services
- PWD-friendly public-facing interfaces

The `ICTO-NCDA Joint Circular No. 1, s. 2010` remains a useful supplementary accessibility source, especially if you want an additional government accessibility issuance.

## Safer Data Privacy Position for Youth Users Aged 15-17

Your note about not collecting the full address is a good instinct and should stay.

A safer and more defensible formulation is:

- `RA 10742 IRR` recognizes `15-17` as `child youth`
- `RA 10173` and its IRR require lawful, transparent, and proportionate processing of personal data
- recent NPC guidance on child-oriented transparency shows that children’s personal data should receive extra protection
- NPC guidance on data subject rights states that parents or guardians assume responsibility for asserting the privacy rights of minors

Because of this, your system design is safer if it:

- collects only necessary data
- uses `barangay` instead of full residential address when the full address is not needed
- provides a clear privacy notice and purpose statement
- applies extra caution when accounts or records involve minors

## Revised Statement of the Problem with RRL and Legal Anchors

Local Youth Development Offices (LYDOs) are legally recognized structures for youth governance under `RA 10742`, as strengthened by `RA 11768`, and are expected to support youth participation, local youth planning, and youth development functions at the local government level. The implementing framework of the law further connects youth planning, youth organizations, and local youth development processes through instruments such as the `Local Youth Development Plan (LYDP)` and the registration and verification of youth and youth-serving organizations under the `2016 IRR of RA 10742`. However, Philippine and Philippines-focused studies suggest that several gaps continue to affect how local youth governance connects with the youth and with the public (Valenzuela, 2025; Bautista, 2020; Castillo et al., 2024).

First, youth access to information and participation opportunities may become inconsistent when communication and engagement depend on fragmented channels rather than a structured system. Studies on youth participation and e-participation in the Philippines show that communication quality, institutional credibility, and the actual design of participation mechanisms affect whether young people are meaningfully reached and engaged (Huffman, 2017; Bautista, 2020; Castillo et al., 2024). This concern is also legally significant because the proposed system operates in a public-service context where digital government is now recognized under `RA 12254` as a citizen-centered, transparent, and integrated mode of service delivery, and where local public information functions are consistent with the intent of the `Local Government Code` and public-service transparency norms.

Second, the monitoring of youth engagement and program outcomes remains a challenge. Research on LYDO operations and Filipino youth leadership shows the need for stronger mechanisms to track participation, assess implementation, and identify areas for improvement in youth programs (Valenzuela, 2025; Reguindin, 2023; Valencia, 2023). This gap is closely connected to the legal design of youth governance itself, since the `2016 IRR of RA 10742` ties youth planning, organization registration, and local youth development functions to more structured local processes. Without a centralized platform, local youth offices may struggle to organize records, monitor engagement patterns, and support evidence-based program decisions.

Third, transparency and public access to governance information may also be weakened when records are not managed in a centralized and accessible digital environment. The `1987 Constitution` recognizes the people’s right to information on matters of public concern and adopts a policy of full public disclosure of transactions involving public interest. This is reinforced by `RA 6713`, which requires that public documents be made accessible to the public, and supported by `EO No. 2, s. 2016`, which operationalizes access-to-information principles in the Executive Branch. Philippine studies likewise connect transparency and accountability with public trust and service delivery, while also showing that access systems can fail when design, responsiveness, and user support are weak (Ong & Gabriel, 2018; Gabriel et al., 2019; Perez, 2020; Tan & Lum, 2025).

These governance concerns are further shaped by legal requirements on digital service design, privacy, and security. `RA 10173` requires lawful, transparent, proportionate, and secure processing of personal data, while `RA 12254` emphasizes citizen-centered e-governance, interoperability, inclusivity, privacy-by-design, and secure online public service platforms. In practical terms, these laws support the need for a youth governance platform that does not merely post information, but also protects user data, minimizes unnecessary personal data collection, and provides a structured mechanism for public access, participation, and accountability. Studies on local digital governance in the Philippines also show that better-designed web systems improve transparency, accessibility, and service delivery compared with fragmented or weakly organized digital practices (Barrios & Moreno, 2024; Albano, 2024; Lagura, 2025).

Taken together, these legal and empirical foundations indicate a broader problem: local youth governance processes may remain fragmented across informal communication, disconnected records, and weakly integrated digital practices, making it difficult to connect information dissemination, youth participation, program monitoring, and transparency in one coherent workflow (Huffman, 2017; Valenzuela, 2025; Perez, 2020; Barrios & Moreno, 2024). This highlights the need for a centralized web-based platform that can provide reliable access to youth information, support participation and monitoring, and strengthen transparency, accountability, privacy, and service responsiveness in local youth governance.
